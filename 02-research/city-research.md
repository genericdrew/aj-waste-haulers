## Dripping Springs, TX

### County Jurisdiction
Hays County. The City of Dripping Springs is the OSSF authorized agent for properties inside city limits. Unincorporated properties in the surrounding area fall under Hays County Development Services (OSSF program, 512-393-2150). Hays County requires permits for ALL on-site sewage facilities regardless of lot size or acreage — there is no minimum-lot exemption that exists in some other Texas counties. Ongoing maintenance contracts are mandatory for all aerobic and advanced-treatment systems in Hays County. No permits are issued for properties that violate Hays County Development (Subdivision) Regulations.
- Sources: [Hays County On-Site Sewage](https://www.hayscountytx.gov/on-site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Hays County Septic Permits](https://www.hayscountytx.gov/septic-permits) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Aquifer Sensitivity — Unique Regulatory Context
Dripping Springs sits atop the Trinity Aquifer recharge zone in western Hays County, managed by the Hays Trinity Groundwater Conservation District (HTGCD). The HTGCD's authority covers the Trinity Aquifer across the western portion of Hays County including Dripping Springs, Driftwood, and Wimberley. New non-exempt commercial and utility wells must bypass the vulnerable Middle Trinity Aquifer and reach the more isolated Lower Trinity Aquifer. Proximity to the Edwards Aquifer Contributing Zone (EACZ) means some properties also trigger TCEQ 30 TAC §285.40 enhanced requirements. The compound effect: septic system design, setback distances, and permitting timelines in Dripping Springs are significantly more complex than in non-aquifer-sensitive Texas counties — adding weeks to the permitting timeline.
- Sources: [Hays Trinity Groundwater Conservation District — Management Zones](https://haysgroundwater.com/management-special-study-zones/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [TCEQ Edwards Aquifer Recharge Zone Rules](http://txrules.elaws.us/rule/title30_chapter285_sec.285.40) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Utility Providers
- **Water:** Dripping Springs Water Supply Corporation (Dripping Springs WSC) serves the broader community area. The City of Dripping Springs owns wastewater infrastructure serving properties inside city limits and customers within select subdivisions with Development Agreements.
- **Wastewater:** The City of Dripping Springs' current wastewater treatment facility is operating at capacity and cannot accept new connections; a new South Regional Wastewater Reclamation Facility has been proposed to treat up to 822,500 GPD. Individual master-planned developments outside city limits often build and operate their own sewage collection/treatment systems under individual TCEQ permits.
- **Sewer vs. Septic:** Properties inside city limits with a Development Agreement may connect to city sewer. All other properties — the large majority of the market area — are on individual OSSF (septic). This includes most rural-acreage residences and the older unincorporated subdivisions west and south of US-290.
- Sources: [Dripping Springs WSC — Area We Serve](https://drippingspringswater.com/area-we-serve) (accessed 2026-04-17); [City of Dripping Springs — Water/Wastewater Service](https://www.cityofdrippingsprings.com/public-works-department/pages/waterwastewater-service) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Construction Eras
Dripping Springs was a community of ~600 residents in the mid-1980s. By 2000, population was 1,548; by 2026, approximately 12,692 — an 11% annual growth rate. The dominant housing construction eras are: (1) pre-1990 rural farmsteads and ranch-division lots on 5–60 acres (oldest septic stock, mostly conventional tanks, due for pumping cycles); (2) 2000–2015 subdivision wave (Belterra, Highpointe, and early Caliterra phases); (3) 2015–present master-planned communities (Caliterra with 2,200+ homes planned, Headwaters). Lot sizes ranged historically from 2–3 acres (rural majority) down to subdivision lots. The city has 8,000+ permitted lots in its development pipeline as of 2025.
- Sources: [Dripping Springs Texas Population History](https://www.biggestuscities.com/city/dripping-springs-texas) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Dripping Springs 2026 Population](https://worldpopulationreview.com/us-cities/texas/dripping-springs) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Dripping Springs Master-Planned Communities Guide](https://ownalittlepieceoftexas.com/blog/A-Guide-to-Master-Planned-Communities-in-Dripping-Springs) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Housing Stock
Mixed: rural-acreage homes (historically 2–60 acre lots, no zoning, private wells and septic) dominate the unincorporated areas west of SH-12. Newer master-planned subdivisions (Belterra built ~2004+, Highpointe, Caliterra circa 2016+, Headwaters circa 2017+) have smaller lots but typically connect to their own community wastewater systems under TCEQ permits or Development Agreements with the city. The rural-acreage stock is the primary residential septic market: older conventional tanks on 2–5 acre parcels are the bread-and-butter pumping customer.
- Sources: [Best Neighborhoods in Dripping Springs](https://lrgrealty.com/lrg-blog/best-neighborhoods-in-dripping-springs-tx) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Dripping Springs Neighborhood Guide](https://www.austinrealestatehomesblog.com/dripping-springs/) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Local Character — Wedding Capital + Beverage Cluster
Dripping Springs was officially designated by the Texas Legislature as the "Wedding Capital of Texas" in 2015. The city grew from one wedding venue to more than 35 within 15 square miles, hosting approximately 1,000 weddings per year — making it one of the highest-density event-venue concentrations in Texas. It has also become the unofficial "Distillery Capital of Texas," with Treaty Oak Distilling (28 acres on Fitzhugh Rd, Texas's 4th distillery ever established, largest certified organic distillery in Texas), Dripping Springs Distilling (Dripping Springs Vodka), Deep Eddy Vodka, Desert Door Texas Sotol, Crowded Barrel Whiskey, and 12 Fox Brewery among the cluster. Twisted X Brewing and Bell Springs Winery round out the beverage corridor along US-290 and RR-12.
- Sources: [Wedding Capital of Texas Designation](https://www.destinationdrippingsprings.com/p/wedding-capital-of-texas) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Treaty Oak Distilling](https://treatyoakdistilling.com/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Dripping Springs Distillery Cluster](https://rockandvinemag.com/2018/09/the-unofficial-distillery-capital-of-texas/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Dripping Springs TX Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dripping_Springs,_Texas) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Commercial Venue Notes (Grease Trap / Beverage Waste Angle)
Every winery, brewery, distillery, and wedding venue within the Dripping Springs unincorporated corridor operates on a private OSSF — there is no municipal sewer in these commercial areas. Treaty Oak Distilling (16604 Fitzhugh Rd), Desert Door (distillery and event space), Twisted X Brewing, and dozens of wedding venues all generate commercial-grade liquid waste — spent mash, yeast slurry, cleaning-in-place (CIP) effluent, and event catering grease — that must be hauled by TCEQ-licensed liquid waste haulers. This is one of the most concentrated clusters of commercial septic and industrial-process liquid waste in the Texas Hill Country. AJ's specialty in winery/brewery/distillery waste removal is directly relevant to this corridor.
- Sources: [Dripping Springs Wineries, Breweries, Distilleries Guide](https://www.destinationdrippingsprings.com/p/stayanddine/sipping-in-dripping) (accessed 2026-04-17); [6 Breweries/Distilleries in Dripping Springs](https://austin.culturemap.com/news/restaurants-bars/09-08-21-whats-brewing-in-dripping-springs/) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Septic-Specific Notes
- Trinity Aquifer sits directly beneath most of the Dripping Springs market area; septic failures or overloads carry groundwater contamination risk far beyond neighboring counties.
- Older rural properties (pre-2000) commonly have conventional tank-and-drainfield systems with no maintenance history; pumping intervals of 3–5 years are typical for 3-bedroom homes on 2–5 acres.
- Permitting timelines for new systems: 3–8 weeks for site evaluation, design, and Hays County Authorization to Construct.
- Commercial aerobic systems (event venues, distilleries) require perpetual maintenance contracts with licensed operators under Hays County OSSF rules.
- Sources: [River Hills Homes — Septic in Hill Country](https://riverhillsbuilder.com/septic-systems-hill-country/) (accessed 2026-04-17)

## Austin, TX

### County Jurisdiction
Travis County. Unique dual-authority structure: Austin Water (City of Austin) is the OSSF authorized agent for properties within the City's full jurisdiction and annexed areas under the Texas Health and Safety Code. Travis County Transportation and Natural Resources (TNR) permits OSSFs for unincorporated Travis County properties not within Austin Water's jurisdiction. LCRA jurisdiction applies to some ETJ and watershed-protection areas. This three-way overlap — Austin Water, Travis County TNR, LCRA — means property owners must verify which authority governs before applying for a permit. Austin Water's OSSF Interactive Map is the definitive tool for jurisdiction lookup.
- Sources: [Austin Water OSSF Program](https://www.austintexas.gov/department/site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Travis County OSSF Permit](https://www.traviscountytx.gov/tnr/development-services/apply-for-a-permit/residential-ossf-septic-system-permit) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### City-Specific OSSF Rules Beyond TCEQ State Requirements
Austin City Code Chapter 15-5 imposes nitrogen-reduction mandates that exceed baseline TCEQ 30 TAC Chapter 285 state standards. Nitrogen-reducing treatment is required for all proposed systems with drainfields in:
1. Within 75 feet of Lake Austin
2. The Edwards Aquifer Recharge Zone (EARZ)
3. Areas of the Barton Springs segment of the Edwards Aquifer Contributing Zone (EACZ) with certain soil conditions

Approved nitrogen-reduction systems must achieve average annual concentrations of 20 mg/L or less total nitrogen (or a maximum of 11 pounds total nitrogen per year). Nitrogen-reducing and commercial aerobic systems require perpetual maintenance contracts — homeowners cannot self-maintain these systems; only manufacturer-trained certified personnel may service them. This Austin-specific rule has no equivalent in the state TCEQ code.
- Sources: [Austin Water OSSF Nitrogen Reduction Systems](https://www.austintexas.gov/page/ossf-nitrogen-reduction-systems) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Austin Water OSSF](https://www.austintexas.gov/water/site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Utility Providers — Sewer vs. Septic
- **Water:** Austin Water serves most of the city and much of the ETJ. Multiple water districts (LCRA member utilities, WCID 17, etc.) serve portions of the western ETJ.
- **Wastewater/Sewer:** Austin Water's wastewater system covers the developed core. However, large portions of southwest and western Austin — particularly areas west of MoPac Expressway, throughout the Barton Creek watershed, and in the Bear Creek/Onion Creek/Slaughter Creek sub-watersheds — are NOT on city sewer and rely on OSSF.
- Documented septic-heavy zones: Bear Creek watershed (highest OSSF permit density in the Barton Springs Zone), Barton Hills, Barton Creek corridor, Far South Austin/Slaughter-Manchaca area, western Travis County unincorporated areas near the Hays County line. At least 9,470 OSSF permits exist in the Barton Springs Zone alone; Travis County has 6,862 additional OSSF permit records dating to 1977.
- Sources: [Austin Water OSSF](https://www.austintexas.gov/department/site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Hill Country Alliance Wastewater Study — Barton Springs Zone OSSF data](https://www.hillcountryalliance.org/uploads/HCA/WastewaterBartonSprings.pdf) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Travis County Edwards Aquifer](https://www.traviscountytx.gov/tnr/environmental-quality/water-quality/edwards-aquifer) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Construction Eras
Austin is the region's metro anchor with a much longer construction history than other service-area cities. Key eras:
- Pre-1970s: Inner-city core, most on municipal sewer.
- 1970s–1990s: Suburban expansion westward (Barton Hills, Circle C, Oak Hill areas), where many properties were built on rural-lot parcels with septic before municipal sewer extension.
- 2000s–present: Continued sprawl into southwest/west Austin ETJ where sewer lines haven't followed development. 59.5% of Travis County OSSF permits are conventional anaerobic systems; 39.2% are aerobic spray systems — indicating a mix of older conventional tanks and more recently installed advanced systems.
- Sources: [Hill Country Alliance Wastewater Study](https://www.hillcountryalliance.org/uploads/HCA/WastewaterBartonSprings.pdf) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Housing Stock
Highly mixed. For AJ's market: the septic-relevant stock concentrates in (1) the western Travis County unincorporated acreage tracts along FM 1826, FM 2244, and Bee Cave Rd corridors; (2) older pre-sewer-extension subdivisions in southwest Austin; (3) rural-residential properties south of Slaughter Lane toward the Hays County line. These areas share characteristics with the Dripping Springs/Driftwood market: 1–5 acre lots, private wells, older conventional septic tanks, limestone-based soils with shallow bedrock making drain field design challenging.
- Sources: [HomeField Travis County — Austin Septic Services](https://www.homefieldonsite.com/travis-county/austin/) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Local Character
Austin is the Travis County seat and metro anchor for the entire Texas Hill Country corridor — home to the University of Texas, a major technology industry cluster (Samsung, Tesla, Apple, Meta campuses), and the state capital. For AJ, Austin represents the largest single market by property count but also the most competitive. The relevant septic sub-market is the outer ring: western and southwestern Austin where urbanization has outpaced sewer infrastructure extension.

### Commercial Venue Notes
Austin has thousands of restaurants, event venues, food trucks, and hospitality businesses generating commercial grease trap waste. Austin Water requires grease traps/interceptors for food service establishments. Restaurants within the city limits on sewer still require licensed grease trap pumping — a significant commercial service volume. Venues outside city sewer, particularly along the Austin–Dripping Springs corridor (RR 620, FM 1826, W. William Cannon Dr corridor), rely on commercial septic + grease interceptor systems that need licensed haulers.

### Septic-Specific Notes
- Edwards Aquifer Contributing Zone overlaps with southern and western Austin; Austin City Code Chapter 15-5 nitrogen mandate applies to new systems in these zones.
- The Barton Springs Segment of the EACZ is particularly sensitive — six watersheds (Barton, Bear, Little Barton, Little Bear, Onion, Slaughter, Williamson) all flow into it.
- At least 1 acre per single-family dwelling is required for OSSFs on the Edwards Aquifer Recharge Zone proper under TCEQ 30 TAC §285.40.
- Austin Water mandates perpetual maintenance contracts for nitrogen-reducing and commercial aerobic systems — a compliance obligation that requires a licensed service provider regardless of whether the homeowner can maintain the system themselves.
- Sources: [TCEQ §285.40 — OSSFs on Edwards Aquifer Recharge Zone](http://txrules.elaws.us/rule/title30_chapter285_sec.285.40) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Austin Water OSSF Nitrogen Reduction](https://www.austintexas.gov/page/ossf-nitrogen-reduction-systems) (accessed 2026-04-17)

## Kyle, TX

### County Jurisdiction
Hays County. The City of Kyle is the largest municipality in Hays County by recent growth trajectory. Properties within the city limits and Kyle's Certificate of Convenience and Necessity (CCN) wastewater service area are served by city sewer — but the CCN does not perfectly match city limits or the ETJ. Several active water utilities operate within Kyle's orbit: the City of Kyle Water Utilities, and portions fall under separate utility districts. Properties outside Kyle's wastewater CCN in unincorporated Hays County must obtain OSSF permits through Hays County Development Services. Kyle city code prohibits OSSF installation if adequate city sewer service is available within 500 feet of the property line.
- Sources: [City of Kyle — Water & Wastewater Services](https://www.cityofkyle.gov/city-services/water-utilities/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Hays County On-Site Sewage](https://www.hayscountytx.gov/on-site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Hays County Septic Permits](https://www.hayscountytx.gov/septic-permits) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Utility Providers
- **Water:** City of Kyle Water Utilities, plus multiple districts in the ETJ.
- **Wastewater:** City of Kyle — currently undergoing a major $250M+ expansion of the Plum Creek Wastewater Treatment Plant from 4.5 MGD to 9.0 MGD (first phase to 6 MGD operational November 2026; full 9 MGD by September 2028, eventually 12 MGD). This expansion acknowledges that rapid growth has pushed the existing system to capacity.
- **Sewer vs. Septic:** The majority of Kyle's established subdivisions are on city sewer. However, the outer edges — rural ETJ properties, older acreage parcels predating urban expansion, and properties where the wastewater CCN hasn't caught up with annexation — are still on septic. Alpha Septic Services lists Plum Creek, Waterleaf, Crosswinds, and Anthem as regular service areas, indicating active septic populations within or adjacent to these subdivisions.
- Sources: [STV — Kyle WWTP Expansion](https://stvinc.com/news/city-of-kyle-advances-250-million-wastewater-expansion-with-stvs-accelerated-design/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Kyle WWTP Expansion Timeline](https://kyletxnews.com/wastewater-treatment-plant-expansion-enters-next-phase/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Alpha Septic — Kyle TX](https://www.callalphaseptic.com/service-areas/kyle-tx-septic-services.html) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Construction Eras — Key Market Intelligence
Kyle's growth story is among the most extreme in the United States:
- **2000 Census:** 5,314 residents
- **2010 Census:** 28,016 residents (+427% in one decade)
- **2020 Census:** 45,697 residents
- **2025 Projected:** ~68,935 residents; growing at ~4.7% annually

**82.3% of Kyle's total housing units were built after 2000.** This is the defining market fact: the vast majority of Kyle's housing stock is 25 years old or less, concentrated in the 2003–2015 boom era. Many of these homes have septic tanks now reaching or approaching the 15–20 year pumping-and-inspection cycle — creating a growing wave of maintenance demand that did not exist a decade ago.

Plum Creek, Kyle's signature master-planned community, began development around 2002 and was named 2006 Master Planned Community of the Year. Full build-out at 2,200 acres targets up to 7,000 residents and 2,500+ homes — many of which, depending on proximity to the city sewer CCN, operate on private septic.
- Sources: [Kyle TX Population by Year — Neilsberg](https://www.neilsberg.com/insights/kyle-tx-population-by-year/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Kyle 2nd Fastest Growing City](https://kyleed.com/about-kyle-ed/news-and-events/kyle-ranked-as-2nd-fastest-growing-city) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Plum Creek Master-Planned Community — HR Green](https://www.hrgreen.com/projects/plum-creek-master-planned-community/) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Housing Stock
Predominantly suburban subdivision housing built in the 2000s and 2010s. Kyle's housing mix includes:
- **Plum Creek** — 2,200-acre master-planned community with new-urbanism design, ~2,500 homes at build-out, began ~2002. A mix of city sewer and septic depending on phase and proximity to infrastructure.
- **Waterleaf, Crosswinds, Anthem** — Subdivisions with documented active septic service populations.
- Rural acreage parcels in the ETJ and unincorporated Hays County — primarily older conventional septic systems.
- Lot sizes in newer subdivisions: 6,000–10,000 sq ft typical. Older/rural: 1+ acres.

Kyle's I-35 position (7 miles south of Austin, 39 miles north of San Antonio) makes it a commuter bedroom community — homeowner demographics skew toward working families with less time for DIY maintenance, creating demand for professional septic services.
- Sources: [Kyle at a Glance — Chamber of Commerce](https://www.kylechamber.org/kyle-at-a-glance) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Plum Creek](https://www.plumcreektx.com/) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Local Character
Kyle is the I-35 growth corridor poster city — faster growth than Austin, cheaper land, and a younger demographic. It hosts Seton Medical Center Kyle, a H-E-B Plus, and increasingly dense retail development along I-35. For AJ: Kyle represents a high-volume residential pumping market where newer tanks (2003–2015 build era) are reaching first-time maintenance cycles and homeowners may never have scheduled a pump-out.

### Commercial Venue Notes
Kyle's commercial base is primarily retail and healthcare along I-35. Restaurant and commercial kitchen grease trap service is an active market as Kyle's dining scene grows with the population. No significant winery/brewery/distillery cluster equivalent to Dripping Springs.

### Septic-Specific Notes
- Hays County OSSF rules apply to all unincorporated Kyle-area properties: permits required regardless of lot size, mandatory aerobic system maintenance contracts.
- Kyle city code: no septic permit issued if city sewer is within 500 feet.
- The 2000s-era construction wave means many conventional septic tanks are now 15–25 years old — the key pumping-cadence window for 3–4 bedroom homes with 2–4 residents.
- The wastewater plant expansion (to 9 MGD by 2028) will extend city sewer coverage, potentially converting some current septic customers to city sewer — a market that will need final pump-outs and system decommissioning service.
- Sources: [City of Kyle Standards and Specifications](https://ecode360.com/45539893) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Hays County OSSF](https://www.hayscountytx.gov/on-site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17)

## Driftwood, TX

### County Jurisdiction
Hays County. Driftwood is an unincorporated community — there is no City of Driftwood government. All OSSF permitting is administered by Hays County Development Services, with the same rules that govern the rest of unincorporated Hays County (permits required for all OSSFs regardless of lot size; mandatory aerobic maintenance contracts). There is no municipal sewer in Driftwood. Every residential and commercial property operates on private septic. This is one of the highest-density commercial septic markets in the Texas Hill Country given the concentration of food-service venues, event centers, wineries, breweries, and distilleries.
- Sources: [Hays County On-Site Sewage](https://www.hayscountytx.gov/on-site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Driftwood, Texas — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Driftwood,_Texas) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Utility Providers
- **Water:** Private wells and small water supply corporations serving the unincorporated area. No municipal water system.
- **Wastewater:** No municipal wastewater system exists. 100% of properties — residential and commercial — are on individual or shared OSSFs. This includes all wineries, breweries, distilleries, restaurants, and event venues.
- Sources: [Hays County On-Site Sewage](https://www.hayscountytx.gov/on-site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Construction Eras
Driftwood's ZIP code (78619) covers approximately 5,819 residents (2026 data) in a historically rural area. Homes in ZIP code 78619 were primarily built in the 1990s and 2000s — reflecting the first wave of Hill Country rural-residential development when Austin's westward sprawl began pushing buyers toward acreage properties. The commercial venue cluster (Salt Lick BBQ opened in original form in the 1960s; wineries and breweries largely 2000s–2015 era) predates the residential boom and has driven the area's identity.

Median home price: $1,130,000 — reflecting premium rural-acreage parcels.
- Sources: [Driftwood TX ZIP Code Demographics](https://www.bestplaces.net/people/zip-code/texas/driftwood/78619) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Driftwood TX City-Data Profile](https://www.city-data.com/city/Driftwood-Texas.html) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Housing Stock
Exclusively rural-acreage. Properties commonly feature well, septic, and electricity with no municipal services. Lot sizes range from 0.5 acres (minimum for OSSF in some configurations) to large ranch parcels of 100+ acres. Notable gated community: Liberty Ranch (15 residential lots, approximately 100 acres each). The Vineyard area and similar developments reflect the high-end rural-acreage character. No conventional suburban subdivision density.

The rural character means older conventional septic tanks (installed 1990s–early 2000s) are common throughout the residential stock — many on a 3–5 year pumping cycle. Commercial properties with high-volume liquid waste (restaurants, wineries, event venues) generate frequent pump-out demand independent of residential schedules.
- Sources: [Driftwood TX Real Estate — LonghornResidential](https://www.longhornresidential.com/driftwood-homes-for-sale/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Driftwood TX Demographics — Point2Homes](https://www.point2homes.com/US/Neighborhood/TX/Driftwood-Demographics.html) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Local Character — Salt Lick + Winery Corridor + AVA
Driftwood is defined by its concentration of destination food, beverage, and event venues along FM 150 and FM 1826 in a zone often marketed as part of the Hill Country wine country. Named venues and their commercial-waste significance:

**Restaurants:**
- **Salt Lick BBQ** (18300 FM 1826) — One of Texas's most famous barbecue destinations. BYOB wine policy; event center on-site. High-volume commercial kitchen grease generation; 100% on private septic/OSSF.

**Wineries:**
- **Duchman Family Winery** — Italian-varietal winery using 100% Texas grapes; tasting room with onsite Trattoria Lisina restaurant. Commercial OSSF required for winery processing and restaurant operations.
- **Driftwood Estate Winery** — 18-acre hilltop property; Bistro on-site. Winery process waste + food service = complex commercial liquid waste stream.
- **Fall Creek Vineyards** (second location) — Tasting room, screened porch, food service.
- **Salt Lick Cellars** — Winery operations adjacent to the BBQ restaurant.

**Breweries:**
- **Twisted X Brewing** — Near FM 150/RR 12 intersection; food truck, live music events, regular high-volume event days.
- **Vista Brewing** — 21-acre property; European-style brewery, farm-to-table restaurant menu, weekend live music. Commercial waste from brewing operations (spent grain slurry, yeast, CIP effluent) requires specialized liquid waste hauling.

**Distilleries:**
- **Desert Door Texas Sotol** — Specialty sotol distillery; named Best Craft Specialty Spirits Distillery by USA Today. Hosts cocktail classes, art events, live music. Distillery process waste (stillage, cleaning effluent) requires TCEQ-permitted liquid waste haulers.

**Event Venues:**
Eventective lists 180 wedding/party venues in the Driftwood ZIP code area. Named venues include: Stonehouse Villa, Thurman's Mansion, Creekside Pavilion, Emerson Acres, Felton Ranch, Wildflower Barn, Greenhouse at Driftwood, Mercer Dancehall (largest dance floor in Hays County). All operate on private septic; large events generate portable sanitation + septic pump-out demand.

- Sources: [Day Trip to Driftwood — Austin Texas](https://www.austintexas.org/austin-insider-blog/blog/post/driftwood/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Ultimate Guide to Driftwood TX](https://www.destinationdrippingsprings.com/p/aboutus/blog/the-ultimate-guide-to-driftwood-tx) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Salt Lick BBQ — Wine Country listing](https://www.winecountry.com/businesses/18889/salt-lick-bbq) (accessed 2026-04-17); [WeddingWire — Driftwood venues](https://www.weddingwire.com/c/tx-texas/driftwood/winery-brewery-weddings/104-vendors.html) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Commercial Venue Notes (Grease Trap / Beverage Waste)
Driftwood has no municipal sewer. Every commercial venue — Salt Lick BBQ, Duchman, Vista Brewing, Desert Door, Twisted X, and all 180+ event venues — operates on private commercial OSSF and requires licensed TCEQ-permitted liquid waste hauling. This is among the densest clusters of commercial septic demand in the entire Texas Hill Country:
- Restaurants: commercial kitchen grease trap pumping (Salt Lick alone serves thousands of guests on peak weekends)
- Wineries: process water, grape pomace wash, CIP effluent
- Breweries: spent grain slurry, yeast, chemical sanitizer waste
- Distilleries: stillage (spent mash), CIP waste, barrel wash
- Event venues: 180+ venues generating concentrated human waste on event days requiring pump-outs of holding tanks and portable sanitation

AJ's explicit specialty in winery/brewery/distillery waste removal is a direct competitive advantage in Driftwood, where generic septic haulers lack the expertise to handle specialized process waste streams.

### Septic-Specific Notes
- 100% of Driftwood is on private OSSF — no sewer exists or is planned.
- Trinity Aquifer recharge zone designation applies across western Hays County including Driftwood; Hays Trinity Groundwater Conservation District oversight.
- Older residential systems (1990s–2000s build era) approaching or past typical pumping cycles.
- The combination of rural-acreage residential stock plus a dense commercial venue cluster creates overlapping demand that few competitors service with the necessary expertise across both segments.
- Sources: [Hays Trinity GCD](https://haysgroundwater.com/) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Hays County OSSF](https://www.hayscountytx.gov/on-site-sewage-facilities) (accessed 2026-04-17)

## New Braunfels, TX

### County Jurisdiction
Primarily Comal County; a portion of the city extends into Guadalupe County. The City of New Braunfels' Health & Food Safety Division is the authorized OSSF agent for properties within city limits under Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 366 and 30 TAC Chapter 285. The Comal County Environmental Health Department (CCEO) administers OSSFs for all unincorporated Comal County properties. Guadalupe County Environmental Health governs properties in the Guadalupe County portion of the ETJ. Properties outside city limits but within the ETJ follow the appropriate county's rules.

Key Comal County OSSF rule: minimum lot size for a septic system with a public water supply is **1 acre** in unincorporated Comal County (compared to 1/2 acre minimum inside New Braunfels city limits). All OSSF designs must be reviewed by a Registered Sanitarian or Professional Engineer before permits are issued.

### Utility Providers
- **Water (city):** New Braunfels Utilities (NBU) is the primary municipal water provider for the city core. NBU supplies 50,000+ acre-feet annually and was under Stage 2 drought restrictions as of late 2024.
- **Water (fringe/rural):** Texas Water Company (TWC) serves western suburban developments including Vintage Oaks and Meyer Ranch. TWC was under Stage 4 drought restrictions (near-total outdoor water ban) for nearly three years through 2024 — a significant utility stress indicator. Green Valley Special Utility District serves areas southeast of the city.
- **Wastewater/Sewer (city):** New Braunfels Utilities provides sewer to the city core. As of April 2026, NBU is in the final phase of a **$7 million, 18-month sewer main rehabilitation project in Historic Gruene** — replacing a deteriorating 12-inch main with an 18-inch main and adding new odor control along Gruene Road, plus replacing aging 6-inch and 8-inch cast-iron siphon lines crossing the Guadalupe River.
- **Sewer vs. Septic:** The majority of the Historic Gruene district and city core are on NBU sewer. Outer subdivisions (Vintage Oaks, Meyer Ranch, rural Comal County, Canyon Lake area communities) are on private septic — including aerobic systems, which require perpetual maintenance contracts.

### Dominant Construction Eras
New Braunfels has one of the longer histories among these cities (founded 1845 by German settlers) but its modern growth is recent and rapid:
- **Pre-1970s:** Historic core, most structures on municipal sewer.
- **1970s–1990s:** Growth from 27,334 (1990) to 36,494 (2000). Development of Canyon Lake-area subdivisions (Canyon Lake Hills, Canyon Lake Village, and 80+ lake-adjacent neighborhoods by the mid-1980s) — most on septic due to rural setting.
- **2000–2010:** Population surged from 36,494 to 57,740 (+58%) — third-fastest growing city in the U.S. for 2010–2020. The 2000s wave produced a large new-construction septic cohort.
- **2010–2020:** Population 57,740 to 90,403 (+57%). Master-planned communities Veramendi (2013 Development Agreement, 2,400 acres, ~1,200 homes complete by 2024) and Mayfair (1,900 acres, broke ground 2022) dominate the new build.
- **47.9% of New Braunfels housing units were built after 2000** (~15,400 units).
- Sources: [New Braunfels Census QuickFacts](https://www.census.gov/quickfacts/newbraunfelscitytexas) (accessed 2026-04-17); [New Braunfels Population History](https://www.biggestuscities.com/city/new-braunfels-texas) (accessed 2026-04-17); [Community Impact — New Braunfels Housing Development 2024](https://communityimpact.com/san-antonio/new-braunfels/development/2024/10/11/new-braunfels-rise-in-housing-developments-pushes-its-infrastructure-services/) (accessed 2026-04-17)

### Dominant Housing Stock
Diverse mix that reflects the city's longer history:
- **Historic city core / Gruene district:** Older homes, predominantly on city sewer, some with aging systems.
- **Canyon Lake area** (unincorporated Comal County, ~30 minutes northwest): 80+ neighborhood subdivisions around the Corps of Engineers lake, nearly all on private septic. Minimum lot size 1 acre per Comal County rules. 1970s–1980s construction era dominates; these are among the oldest private septic systems in AJ's service area.
- **Vintage Oaks at the Vineyard** — upscale Hill Country acreage community; aerobic surface irrigation systems documented (e.g., a Vintage Oaks Lot 1595 aerobic system permitted 07/30/2024 by Comal County). On Texas Water Company for water; private septic for wastewater.
- **Meyer Ranch** — master-planned, Highway 46 corridor, some septic + water district.
- **Veramendi** — created its own Water Improvement District; connected to NBU for sewer in phases. Some septic during build-out phases.
- **Mayfair** — own water improvement district; 1,900 acres, 2022 groundbreak.

### Local Character
New Braunfels' identity centers on three assets: (1) the **Guadalupe and Comal Rivers** — the Comal River is the shortest river in Texas and the coldest springfed river in the Southwest, making New Braunfels a year-round river recreation destination; (2) **Schlitterbahn Waterpark** — originally opened 1979 on the Comal River, the fourth most-visited waterpark in the country, drawing nearly 1 million visitors annually from mid-April through mid-September; (3) **Historic Gruene** — a National Register of Historic Places district (listed 1975) anchored by Gruene Hall (Texas's oldest continuously operating dance hall, c. 1878), the Gristmill River Restaurant, and Gruene Mansion Inn. The German founding heritage (1845) remains visible in architecture, street names, and the Wurstfest festival (November).

Population: approximately 128,661 as of 2022 data; the 2020 Census recorded 90,403. New Braunfels is now approaching build-out capacity within its city limits.

### Commercial Venue Notes
New Braunfels has a dense hospitality and event sector driven by river tourism, Schlitterbahn, and the Gruene Historic District:
- Gruene Hall and surrounding district restaurants/bars — all on NBU sewer following the 2026 sewer main rehabilitation, but aging infrastructure previously generated frequent overflow events.
- Schlitterbahn Waterpark — major commercial food service grease trap generation.
- River tubing outfitters, riverside restaurants (Gristmill, etc.) — commercial kitchen waste.
- Wineries and event venues in unincorporated Comal County (west of the city on Highway 46 corridor, near Spring Branch and Fischer area) — all on private septic.

### Septic-Specific Notes
- Comal County OSSF minimum: 1 acre lot with public water supply for septic.
- Canyon Lake area is a high-priority septic market: 80+ subdivisions, 1970s–1980s construction era, systems potentially 40–50 years old; the Guadalupe River proximity creates strict setback enforcement.
- Guadalupe-Blanco River Authority (GBRA) has jurisdiction over some areas south of Klein Rd for water supply — a different utility than NBU.
- The NBU $7M Gruene sewer main rehabilitation (January–May 2026) indicates aging wastewater infrastructure even in the well-served city core; commercial properties along Gruene Road may experience service disruptions during the project.
- Vintage Oaks aerobic systems require perpetual maintenance contracts under Comal County/state TCEQ rules — a compliance obligation requiring a licensed service provider.
